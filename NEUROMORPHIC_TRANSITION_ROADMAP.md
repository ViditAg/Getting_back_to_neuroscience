# Neuromorphic Computing Transition Roadmap

## Executive Summary

This document outlines a strategic roadmap for transitioning from Optical Proximity Correction (OPC) modeling at Intel to neuromorphic computing research, with a focus on collaboration with the University of Oregon's Institute of Neuroscience, eventual patent development, and company formation.

**Your Unique Value Proposition:**
- PhD in Computational Neuroscience
- Industry experience in advanced semiconductor modeling (OPC at Intel)
- Deep understanding of both biological neural systems and computational modeling
- Experience with precision engineering and manufacturing constraints

---

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (Months 0-6)

### Knowledge Refresh & Skill Development

#### 1. Neuromorphic Computing Fundamentals
- **Week 1-4: Core Concepts**
  - Review neuromorphic computing principles (spiking neural networks, event-based processing)
  - Study key papers:
    - Mead's "Neuromorphic Electronic Systems" (foundational)
    - Recent reviews in Nature Electronics and Nature Machine Intelligence
  - Platforms: Intel Loihi, IBM TrueNorth, BrainScaleS, SpiNNaker

- **Week 5-8: Hardware & Algorithms**
  - Memristor technology and emerging memory devices
  - Spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) implementations
  - Event-based sensors (DVS cameras, silicon cochleas)
  - Tools: Brian2, NEST, BindsNET, Nengo

- **Week 9-12: Applications**
  - Edge AI and low-power computing
  - Robotics and autonomous systems
  - Real-time sensory processing
  - Adaptive learning systems

#### 2. Bridge Your OPC Experience
**Key Transferable Skills:**
- Computational lithography → Neuromorphic chip fabrication constraints
- Pattern optimization → Neural network topology optimization
- Inverse problems solving → Learning algorithm design
- High-performance computing → Large-scale neural simulation

**Potential Research Angles:**
- Applying OPC optimization techniques to neuromorphic chip layout
- Lithography-aware neuromorphic architecture design
- Manufacturing variability in analog neuromorphic systems
- Defect tolerance in neuromorphic hardware

## Phase 3: Patent Development (Months 12-24)

### Patent Strategy

#### 1. Areas for Patent Development
Based on your unique position:

- **Hardware Architecture Patents:**
  - Lithography-optimized neuromorphic layouts
  - Variability-compensating circuit designs
  - Novel synapse or neuron circuit implementations
  - Hybrid analog-digital architectures

- **Algorithm Patents:**
  - Learning algorithms optimized for hardware constraints
  - Mapping techniques from conventional to neuromorphic
  - Energy-efficient inference methods
  - Real-time adaptation algorithms

- **Application Patents:**
  - Neuromorphic solutions for specific industries
  - Manufacturing process monitoring using neuromorphic sensors
  - Edge AI for semiconductor inspection
  - Robotics control systems

#### 2. Patent Development Process

**Month 12-15: Prior Art Search & Disclosure**
- Conduct thorough patent landscape analysis
- Tools: Google Patents, USPTO, Espacenet, Lens.org
- Focus areas: neuromorphic hardware, spiking networks, event-based processing
- Identify white spaces and opportunities

**Month 15-18: Invention Disclosures**
- Document 3-5 inventions from your research
- Work with UO Technology Transfer Office
- Coordinate with Intel IP policies (important!)
- Consider provisional patents for speed

**Month 18-21: Patent Applications**
- File provisional patents (less expensive, 12-month window)
- Use university's patent attorneys
- Budget: $5-15K per patent for provisionals
- Full utility patents: $15-30K each

**Month 21-24: Patent Portfolio Development**
- Convert provisionals to full utility patents
- File international (PCT) applications if applicable
- Build portfolio of 3-5 patents minimum for company formation

#### 3. IP Ownership Considerations

**Critical Issues to Address:**
- **Intel IP:** Ensure your patents don't conflict with Intel's claims
  - Review your employment agreement
  - Disclose potential conflicts early
  - Work done outside Intel, on own time, own resources = typically yours
  - Consider taking sabbatical or unpaid leave for patent development

- **University IP:** Negotiate ownership with UO
  - Standard: University owns, licenses to inventors
  - Negotiate favorable licensing terms upfront
  - Exclusive vs. non-exclusive licenses
  - Equity in lieu of licensing fees

- **Joint IP:** If collaboration with UO faculty
  - Joint ownership agreements
  - Clear commercialization path
  - Fair equity split in future company

  - Set up cap table
  - Draft employee IP agreements

- **Legal Costs:** $10-25K
- **Lawyers:** Look for startup-focused IP attorneys (Portland, Seattle, Bay Area)

#### 2. Exit Intel (Timing Critical)
- **Options:**
  - **Clean Break:** Resign before major company work
    - Pros: Clean IP, full focus
    - Cons: Income risk, lose benefits
  
  - **Gradual Transition:** Reduce to part-time
    - Pros: Continued income, lower risk
    - Cons: Potential IP complications, split focus
  
  - **Intel Support:** Partner with Intel
    - Pros: Resources, validation, network
    - Cons: Loss of independence, IP sharing

- **Recommendation:** Start company while at Intel (minimal time commitment), resign when funding secured

  - Milestone payments

- **Budget:** $5-10K for licensing setup

- **Risk:** Neuromorphic hardware doesn't achieve promised benefits

- **Risk:** Market not ready for neuromorphic computing

- **Risk:** Patent conflicts with Intel or other parties

- **Mitigation:** SBIR/STTR grants, lean startup approach, maintain Intel job longer
