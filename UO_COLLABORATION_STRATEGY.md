# University of Oregon Collaboration Strategy

## Immediate Action Plan (Week 1-4)

### Week 1: Research & Preparation
**Day 1-2: Institute of Neuroscience (ION) Research**
- Visit: https://ion.uoregon.edu/
- Review faculty directory and research areas
- Focus on:
  - Computational neuroscience faculty
  - Systems neuroscience researchers
  - Anyone working on neural computation, learning, or modeling

**Day 3-4: Computer Science & Engineering Research**
- Computer Science: https://cs.uoregon.edu/
- Look for faculty in:
  - Machine learning
  - Neural networks
  - Hardware/software co-design
  - High-performance computing

**Day 5-7: Create Target List**
- Identify 7-10 potential collaborators
- For each person, document:
  - Name and position
  - Research interests
  - Recent publications (last 2 years)
  - Active grants (if visible)
  - Potential alignment with your background
  - Specific collaboration ideas

### Week 2: Deep Dive on Target Researchers

**For each target researcher:**
1. **Read 3-5 Recent Papers**
   - Understand their methodology
   - Note open questions or future directions
   - Identify how your skills could contribute

2. **Check Seminar Schedules**
   - ION seminars: Usually Fridays
   - CS colloquia
   - Physics seminars
   - Add to your calendar

3. **Explore Their Networks**
   - Co-authors (potential secondary contacts)
   - Graduate students (future collaborators)
   - Funding sources (alignment opportunities)

### Week 3: Craft Outreach Materials

**1. Create Professional Materials:**
- Update LinkedIn profile
  - Emphasize computational neuroscience background
  - Highlight Intel technical achievements
  - Add "Transitioning to Neuromorphic Computing" to headline

- Create 1-page CV focused on:
  - PhD research highlights
  - Relevant Intel projects (non-confidential)
  - Publications in neuroscience
  - Technical skills relevant to neuromorphic computing

- Draft personal website or GitHub profile
  - Research interests
  - Past work samples
  - Contact information

## Collaboration Models

### 1. Informal Collaboration (Months 1-6)
**Structure:**
- Email/Zoom discussions
- Share ideas and preliminary results
- Joint reading of papers
- No formal agreement needed

**Benefits:**
- Low commitment, explore fit
- Build relationship and trust
- Test working compatibility

**Action Items:**
- Schedule monthly video calls
- Share draft papers for feedback
- Discuss potential projects

### 2. Visiting Scholar/Affiliate (Months 3-12)
**Structure:**
- Unpaid affiliation with UO
- Library and resource access
- Attend seminars and events
- Collaborate on research

**How to Arrange:**
- Ask faculty sponsor to nominate you
- Department approval usually required
- Minimal paperwork

**Benefits:**
- Official status for collaboration
- Access to university resources
- Attend restricted events
- Build visibility

**Action Items:**
- Identify faculty sponsor
- Complete application process
- Plan regular campus visits

### 3. Paid Collaboration (Months 6-18)
**Options:**

**A. Consultant/Contractor**
- You provide specific expertise
- Paid hourly or project-based
- Clear deliverables
- IP agreement needed

**B. Grant Collaboration**
- Co-PI or senior personnel on grant
- Part-time effort (5-20%)
- University processes payment
- Joint research outcomes

**C. Industry Partnership**
- Intel sponsors research
- You as technical lead
- Equipment or funding from Intel
- Benefits both parties

**Action Items:**
- Develop grant proposals together
- Explore Intel partnership programs
- Define IP ownership upfront

### 4. Adjunct/Courtesy Appointment (Months 12-24)
**Structure:**
- Part-time teaching appointment
- One course per term (or less)
- Research collaboration expected
- Usually unpaid or modest stipend

**Benefits:**
- Official faculty status
- Can co-advise students
- Vote in department matters
- Strengthen relationship

**Requirements:**
- Department approval
- Dean/provost approval
- Teaching portfolio
- Faculty sponsor

**Action Items:**
- Propose specialized course (e.g., "Neuromorphic Computing Systems")
- Prepare course outline
- Apply through faculty sponsor

## Relationship Management

### 1. Communication Best Practices

**Frequency:**
- Active projects: Weekly updates
- Planning phase: Bi-weekly check-ins
- Maintenance: Monthly touchpoints

**Methods:**
- Email for formal communication
- Slack/messaging for quick questions
- Video calls for discussions
- In-person for brainstorming

**Documentation:**
- Shared Google Docs for ideas
- GitHub for code
- Overleaf for papers
- Regular meeting notes

### 2. Managing Expectations

**Be Clear About:**
- Your time availability
- Intel constraints and obligations
- IP considerations
- Publication timelines
- Long-term goals

**Under-Promise, Over-Deliver:**
- Set realistic deadlines
- Build in buffer time
- Communicate delays early
- Surprise with early completion

### 3. Conflict Resolution

**Potential Issues:**
- Authorship disputes
- Credit for ideas
- IP ownership
- Time commitments
- Resource allocation

**Prevention:**
- Document agreements early
- Regular communication
- Clear expectations
- Written MOUs when appropriate

**Resolution:**
- Address issues promptly
- Seek win-win solutions
- Involve department if needed
- Maintain professionalism

## Conclusion

Building successful collaborations with University of Oregon requires:

1. **Authentic Interest:** Genuine engagement with their research
2. **Clear Value Proposition:** What you bring to the table
3. **Patience:** Relationships take time to develop
4. **Persistence:** Follow up, stay engaged, don't give up
5. **Professionalism:** Respect their time and priorities
6. **Mutual Benefit:** Win-win collaboration model

Your unique background makes you an attractive collaborator. Focus on building genuine relationships, contributing value, and creating opportunities for mutual success.

Start with the Week 1 action items and adjust based on responses and opportunities. Good luck!
